Burning Man, Black Rock City, Nevada

Share on facebookShare on twitterShare on emailShare on pinterest_shareMore Sharing Services

Welcome to my essay poem about Burning Man, where I found my people.

 

DECIDING TO GO. Finally decided to do it, after 20 years of knowing about Burning Man. The woman who guided my first ever film shoot directed the first Burning Man documentary (back in the early 90s, it was 300 people).

After years of saying “next year, maybe,” my friend Leon Feingold announced the launch of our NYC Burning Man Camp, New York Dangerous.

 

I GOT INTO ACTION. Bought plane tickets and volunteered my organizational skills. With no ticket in hand, I led weekly calls with Leon and our fellow campers. (Leon found me a ticket.)

We, NYC Burners and Burgins (=Burner Virgin) raised money for New York Dangerous and my Leukemia & Lymphoma fundraising for the 2013 NYC Marathon, at a movie slumber party. We hosted it with Carlos Montoya at unionsquareloft.com. One of my dear campmates, Rich, found us there – the invite was picked up on public list serves!

 

GETTING MY STUFF THERE. I was traveling directly from London / my cousin Jo’s wedding, to Reno/Black Rock City. It took some brilliant logic to fit my stuff into a 16 sq ft space, on a container traveling from NYC.

 

So finally, I was Packed. For. Burning Man.

 

FINALLY THERE. Here are some glimpses of the Burning Man 2013 I experienced.

DAY 1. Driving from 70 mph from Reno, to 5 mph on the playa in Black Rock City, it felt like I had landed on the moon in my own hovercraft, after hours of shopping, driving, and waiting at the gate. For 4 hours.

 

Once past the gate, it took another 4 hours to find my carmate’s camp and drop her off; find my container camp and pick up my load; find Daithi’s camp, Flamin’ Merkin, and find out where he might be; then set up my tent at New York Dangerous, 4:10 & G.

 

I arrived at camp past midnight–and found I still had energy. Do I sleep like a normal person? Or do I find out what this crazy party is about?

 

Thanks to campmate Blackfeet, who generously lent me his bike, I went out immediately to the happening spots in the desert — Esplanade and the open playa.

I searched for my friends and they were nowhere to be found.
Instead, I found the most incredible music I’d heard in decades. From camp to camp, under the desert twilight, I forgot about them.

Then, after biking 2 miles across the playa, stopping here and there, I found an art car, Dancetronauts. It was like a Disney float, but instead of Mickey and Goofy and princesses, were a DJ and two dancing girls. I parked my bike, my headlight shining into the dancing crowd.

“You need to turn your head lamp off…

btw, I love you. Do you want to dance?”

 

There, I met a fellow soul  on a mission for love - who became my playa guide for the rest of the week .

Welcome home.

And the sun rose, to Robot Heart camp’s inspiring and hopeful rhythms.

 

A WEEK OF AMAZING. That’s all I’m going to say here.

 

IN SUMMARY. Art, ritual, living spaces, music. Love.

I experienced a culture of love and expression, so shamelessly open that the regular world looks different.

 

Burning Man is an adult sleepover camp version of my NYC, where we created a city that Disneyland wished it could be –
where the visitors themselves create the city, the theme, the vision,
by leading and propelling its activity,
to express their heart with more authentically than they ever have yet,
as we transform each others’ lives.

 

I came back and saw that NYC is a “playa” ( = desert, from Spanish for beach) without dust. And I still feel the love . In fact, it is revealed to me.
I am in a new NYC as a result.

Thanks Burning Man and my community there. I love you.

 

PHILOSOPHY.

Burning, to me, is getting up to ride my bike across the open playa, the dust flying all around me- reflectively framing the colorful, the vitally creative, costumes, art, living spaces, and most of all, the people-

on bikes, walking, giving, loving, waiting for me, ready to love, explore, and partner on tasks & adventures we were about to share that day, that evening, into the next daybreak.

That is life, is it not?

 

The BM community isn’t the same without the playa dust. It connects us like the sky.
We created stages, dancefloors, bike paths with the dust, and struggled to deal with this alkaline dust as it covered and reacted with our bodies.

 

You know you’ve left the playa and connection when the dust begins, albeit slowly,
to seep away from first your skin, clothing, bags, car-
clinging so the memory and energy are gently integrated.

 

I’m still tingling from the process.
